<p><b>Higher</b>, <b>post-secondary</b>, <b>tertiary</b> or <b>third level education</b> is the stage of <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a> that occurs at <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academies</a>, <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">colleges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seminary" title="Seminary">seminaries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_technology" title="Institute of technology">institutes of technology</a>. Higher <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> also includes certain college-level institutions, such as <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">vocational schools</a>, trade schools, and career colleges, that award <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">academic degrees</a> or <a href="/wiki/Professional_certification" title="Professional certification">professional certifications</a>.</p>
<p>The right of access to <b>higher education</b> is mentioned in a number of <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international human rights instruments</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a> of 1966 declares, in Article 13, that "higher education shall be made equally accessible to all, on the basis of capacity, by every appropriate means, and in particular by the progressive introduction of free education". In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, Article 2 of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">First Protocol to the European Convention on Human Rights</a>, adopted in 1950, obliges all signatory parties to guarantee the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_education" title="Right to education">right to education</a>.</p>

<p>Higher education is an educational level that follows a completion of a school providing a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/High_school" title="High school">high school</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary school</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">gymnasium</a>. Tertiary education is normally taken to include <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate education</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational education</a> and training. <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">Colleges</a>, <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_technology" title="Institute of technology">institutes of technology</a> are the main institutions that provide tertiary education (sometimes known collectively as tertiary institutions). Examples of institutions that provide post-secondary education are <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">vocational schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Community_college" title="Community college">community colleges</a>, independent colleges (e.g. institutes of technology), and <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the institutes of <a href="/wiki/Technical_and_further_education" title="Technical and further education">technical and further education</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, pre-university <a href="/wiki/College_education_in_Quebec" title="College education in Quebec">colleges</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/IEK" title="IEK">IEKs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. They are sometimes known collectively as tertiary institutions. Completion of a tertiary education program of study generally results in the awarding of <a href="/wiki/Academic_certificate" title="Academic certificate">certificates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diploma" title="Diploma">diplomas</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">academic degrees</a>.</p>

<p>Higher education includes teaching, research, exacting applied work (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Medical_school" title="Medical school">medical schools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dental_school" title="Dental school">dental schools</a>), and social services activities of universities. Within the realm of teaching, it includes both the <i><a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduate</a></i> level, and beyond that, <i><a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">graduate-level</a></i> (or <i>postgraduate</i> level). The latter level of education is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate school</a>, especially in North America.</p>
<p>In many developed countries, a high proportion of the population (up to 50%), now enter higher education at some time in their lives. Higher education is therefore very important to national <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economies</a>, both as a significant industry in its own right and as a source of trained and educated personnel for the rest of the economy. College educated workers command a significant wage premium and are much less likely to become unemployed than less educated workers.<sup id="cite_ref-Simkovic_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simkovic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OECD_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There are two types of higher education in the U.K.: higher academic education, and higher <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational education</a>. Higher education in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> specifically refers to post-secondary institutions that offer <a href="/wiki/Associate%27s_degree" title="Associate's degree" class="mw-redirect">Associate's degrees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">Bachelor's degrees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">Master's degrees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_Specialist" title="Education Specialist" class="mw-redirect">Education Specialist</a> (Ed.S.) degrees or <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> (Ph.D.) degrees, or their equivalents, and also higher professional degrees in areas such as law, medicine, optometry, and dentistry.</p>
<p>Such institutions may also offer non-degree certificates, which indicate completion of a set of courses comprising a body of knowledge on a particular topic, but the granting of such certificates is not the primary purpose of the institutions. <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">Tertiary education</a> is not a term used in reference to post-secondary institutions in the United States or Canada.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reading.2C_mathematics.2C_and_writing">Reading, mathematics, and writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reading, mathematics, and writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Demonstrated ability in reading, mathematics, and writing, as typically measured in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/SAT" title="SAT">SAT</a> or similar tests such as the <a href="/wiki/ACT_(test)" title="ACT (test)">ACT</a>, have often replaced colleges' individual entrance exams, and is often required for admission to higher education.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> There is some question as to whether advanced mathematical skills or talent are in fact necessary for fields such as history, English, philosophy, or art.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT72812_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT72812-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The general higher education and training that takes place in a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>, <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a>, or Institute of Technology usually includes significant <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theoretical</a> and abstract elements, as well as applied aspects (although limited offerings of <a href="/wiki/Internship" title="Internship">internships</a> or <a href="/wiki/Summer_Undergraduate_Research_Fellowship_(SURF)" title="Summer Undergraduate Research Fellowship (SURF)" class="mw-redirect">SURF</a> programs attempt to provide practical applications). In contrast, the vocational higher education and training that takes place at <a href="/wiki/Vocational_university" title="Vocational university">vocational universities and schools</a> usually concentrates on <a href="/wiki/Practice_(learning_method)" title="Practice (learning method)">practical applications</a>, with very little theory.</p>
<p>In addition, <a href="/wiki/Professional" title="Professional">professional</a>-level education is always included within Higher Education, and usually in <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">graduate schools</a>, since many <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines" title="List of academic disciplines" class="mw-redirect">academic disciplines</a> are both vocationally, professionally, and theoretically/research oriented, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary medicine</a>. A basic requirement for entry into these graduate-level programs is almost always a bachelor's degree. Requirements for admission to such high-level graduate programs is extremely competitive, and admitted students are expected to perform well.</p>

<p>In the United States, there are large differences in wages and employment associated with different degrees. Medical doctors and lawyers are generally the highest paid workers, and have among the lowest unemployment rates. Among undergraduate fields of study, science, technology, engineering, math, and business generally offer the highest wages and best chances of employment, while education, communication, and liberal arts degrees generally offer lower wages and a lower likelihood of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-Simkovic_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simkovic-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocational">Vocational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vocational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Higher vocational education and training takes place at the non-university tertiary level. Such education combines teaching of both practical skills and theoretical expertise. Higher education differs from other forms of post-secondary education such as that offered by institutions of <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">vocational education</a>, which are more colloquially known as <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">trade schools</a>. Higher vocational education might be contrasted with education in a usually broader <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> field, which might concentrate on <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a> and abstract <a href="/wiki/Concept" title="Concept">conceptual</a> knowledge.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_employers">As employers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher_education&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: As employers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Universities may employ a number of people. Depending on the funding, a university typically hires one teacher per 3-25 students. According to the ideal of research-university, the university teaching staff is actively involved in the research of the institution. In addition, the university usually also has dedicated research staff and a considerable support staff. Typically to work in higher education as a member of the academic <a href="/wiki/Faculty_(teaching_staff)" title="Faculty (teaching staff)" class="mw-redirect">faculty</a>, a candidate must first obtain a doctorate in an academic field, although some lower teaching positions require only a master's degree.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Most of the administrative staff works in different administrative sections, such as <a href="/wiki/Student_Affairs" title="Student Affairs" class="mw-redirect">Student Affairs</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This appears 'top of the head'. Really needs citation and more university 'sections'. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, there may be central support units, such as a university <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">library</a> which have a dedicated staff.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The professional field involving the collection, analysis, and reporting of higher education data is called <a href="/wiki/Institutional_research" title="Institutional research">institutional research</a>. Professionals in this field can be found at locations in addition to universities, e.g. state educational departments.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
